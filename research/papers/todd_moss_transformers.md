# Power quality in donor-funded infrastructure projects

**Authors:** Susanna Berkouwer, Eric Hsu, Edward Miguel, and Catherine Wolfram
**Date:** Feb 07, 2023
**Source:** Energy for Growth Hub

## Overview
Governments and foreign aid institutions routinely finance large infrastructure construction projects in developing and emerging markets. In 2015, the Government of Kenya launched the Last Mile Connectivity Project (LMCP), designed to connect all Kenyan households to electricity by 2022 using financing from the World Bank (WB) and the African Development Bank (AfDB).

## Key Findings
1. **Timing:** Households in villages funded by the AfDB are connected to electricity on average **8 months sooner** than households in villages funded by the WB. This delay is caused by increased bureaucratic requirements and poor coordination in the WB's unbundled contracting approach.
2. **Quantity:** More poles and household connections were constructed at AfDB sites, partly due to the WB's stricter adherence to the 600m transformer radius rule.
3. **Quality:** WB processes generate tangible benefits: on-the-ground construction quality is **0.6 standard deviations higher** at WB sites. This includes better use of pole caps, stays/struts, and grounding wires.
4. **Longevity:** While the WB's higher quality didn't immediately improve voltage quality, it is likely to improve grid longevity and lower long-term maintenance costs.

## Policy Implications
The optimal contracting structure depends on the project's goals. Governments looking for rapid short-term electrification may prefer bundled (turn-key) methods, whereas those prioritizing long-term durability may prefer unbundled methods with rigorous inspections.
