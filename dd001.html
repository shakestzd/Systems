AI Valuations vs. Infrastructure Spending

The seven largest US tech companies added $ trillion in combined market value since January 2023. Against that, six of the biggest AI infrastructure builders spent $B on capital expenditure in 2025. The question is whether the spending is backed by real revenue, or whether companies are building well ahead of what customers will actually pay for.

Nvidia added +$T — the largest gain of the seven, and larger than Germany's GDP. Nvidia doesn't own a data center or run a cloud; it makes the chips the others buy. Its valuation rests entirely on demand for H100 and Blackwell GPUs continuing to accelerate, which depends on every hyperscaler maintaining its current spend trajectory.

Alphabet added +$T while already spending more on infrastructure than Google Cloud earns. Its AI bet is partly funded from search advertising — $B per quarter from the same engine that AI could eventually disrupt.

Apple added +$T with almost none of the infrastructure spend. At $9.4B in FY2024 capex, it routes complex AI queries to OpenAI and Google rather than building GPU clusters — participating in the AI story without the balance sheet exposure.

Meta added +$T after laying off roughly a quarter of its workforce in 2022–2023. It is now one of the most aggressive spenders, with 2026 guidance at $B — about 80% above what it spent in 2024.

Amazon added +$T and crossed the line in 2025 where infrastructure spending now exceeds what AWS earns — $B on capex against roughly $90B in annual AWS revenue.

Microsoft added +$T. What doesn't show in its reported capex: in three months (Sep–Nov 2025) it signed over $B in off-balance-sheet leases with third-party data center operators — none of it appearing in the figures above.

Tesla added +$T on AI expectations rather than the car business. Investors pay about 160× expected earnings per share, versus 6× for a typical auto company. It spent $5B on AI infrastructure in 2024 and plans $20B+ in 2026.

Combined, the seven added $T against $B in 2025 capex. That gap is either the market correctly pricing a multi-decade infrastructure buildout, or the distance between hope and physics. The rest of this article traces how much of the declared spending converts to durable physical assets.

How did we get here

The current wave is not just large — it is structurally different from the last one. The previous cycle of cloud infrastructure build-out (2015–2021) roughly doubled spending every six years. This one has doubled in two.

That aggregate figure is concentrated in a small number of companies. Not all of them are building for the same reason or at the same rate, and the 2026 guidance varies considerably by company.

Who is building — and how much

Amazon leads the cumulative build-out since 2022. The 2026 guidance column (hatched) shows where companies say they are heading — though that figure carries a ±% uncertainty band based on how much individual companies have revised guidance in recent years.

Those figures understate the real commitment. Alongside direct capital investment, companies have been financing infrastructure through long-term leases and special purpose vehicles. These arrangements are classified as operating expenses rather than capital investment, so they never appear in the totals above.

In three months — September through November 2025 — Microsoft signed over $B in leases with third-party data center operators: Nebius ($B), Nscale ($B), Iren ($B), plus Lambda. None of it shows up in reported capex. Meta financed $B of Louisiana data center construction through a separate entity called Beignet Investor LLC, with Blue Owl Capital providing 80% of the funding.

A note on accounting: US rules that took effect in 2019 require operating leases over 12 months to appear on the balance sheet as assets and liabilities — but they still flow through operating expenses rather than capital investment lines, which is why they disappear from the reported figures.

Revenue vs Capital

Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft each report their cloud businesses as separate segments — AWS, Google Cloud, and Intelligent Cloud — which makes a direct comparison possible: you can see both what they spent and what came back.

Alphabet crossed the line first — spending more on infrastructure than Google Cloud earns — in 2024, and has kept climbing. Amazon followed in 2025. Microsoft, whose Azure revenue base is the largest of the three, is still below 1.0 on a quarterly basis, but its ratio is rising fastest.

One Spending Line, Six Demand Assumptions

There is a deeper problem beneath the revenue gap: the companies building this infrastructure are not all building it for the same reason. A September 2025 survey (NYT, Metz & Weise) found at least six distinct visions being pursued simultaneously, ranging from proven revenue today to long-horizon bets with no commercial model yet.

The same wave of capital investment is funding all six of these theses simultaneously. They imply very different revenue trajectories, different timelines to payback, and different responses if AI demand disappoints.

Guidance Has Been Wrong Before — in Both Directions

The 2026 guidance figure of $B is a management estimate with a historical track record of ±%. Meta cut guidance sharply as its ad business contracted in 2022–2023. Microsoft subsequently raised guidance as Azure demand accelerated faster than expected. Both companies moved by roughly a third.

What Happens in 2026

The numbers look different depending on which revenue scenario proves out.

In the bull case, cloud revenue growth absorbs the spending and the capex-to-revenue ratio starts to normalize. In the base case, the gap persists roughly at its current level. In the bear case — a guidance cut of the size Meta executed in 2022 — both capex and revenue numbers decline, but the ratio stays elevated because demand shortfall drives both sides down together.

The $ that Nvidia's market cap lost in one day after DeepSeek R1's release in January 2025 suggests that markets understand the fragility of this structure. The current valuation depends on each major hyperscaler maintaining its current spending trajectory, and on AI demand scaling faster than the evidence currently supports.

The spending is real. The physical consequences — what it builds, where, and who ends up paying for it — are traced in the rest of this series.
