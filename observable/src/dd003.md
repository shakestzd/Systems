# AI Capital and the Labor Market

<div class="prose-lead">

The AI-and-jobs debate lives in software. Will language models displace knowledge workers? Will agents replace customer service teams? Those questions matter, but they are not where the current labor market signal is clearest.

The clearest signal is in concrete, copper, and conduit. Every dollar of AI capital expenditure eventually lands on a construction site, and every construction site needs licensed electricians. The people building the AI economy are not, mostly, the people you read about in the AI economy.

## Two Labor Markets, One Capital Flow

By ${stats.latest_date ?? "late 2025"}, Computer Systems Design employment had risen **${stats.tech_change ?? "—"}%** from its January 2020 level. Construction grew **${stats.const_change ?? "—"}%** from the same baseline. The aggregate construction number includes housing, commercial, and infrastructure. The data center signal is buried in it.

But in the counties where data centers are actually being built — Loudoun County in Virginia, Maricopa County in Arizona, Licking County in Ohio — construction employment grew far faster than the national average. The two labor markets are connected by the same capital flow and by a shared constraint: a large hyperscale data center campus requires roughly 300 to 600 licensed electricians for 24 to 36 months of construction.

## The Wage Signal

Wages reveal what aggregate employment data cannot: where supply is failing to keep pace with demand. Every occupation in the AI labor stack saw wages rise between 2019 and 2024. The question is which ones are rising because of structural undersupply versus broad-based demand growth.

```js
import { createWageSlopes } from "./js/charts/wage-slopes.js";

const wageChart = createWageSlopes(wages, stats);
// Callout upper-center — the slope lines span the full width (2019 to 2023),
// but the upper quarter of the chart stays clear above the highest-paid occupations.
display(mountScrollChart(wageChart.node, wageChart.update, [
  { prose: "15 occupations in the AI labor stack. Every one saw wages rise.", position: { top: "18%", left: "28%", maxWidth: "24ch" } },
  { prose: `Software Developers: $${stats.sw_wage_2019 ? (stats.sw_wage_2019/1000).toFixed(0) : 105}K to $${stats.sw_wage_2024 ? (stats.sw_wage_2024/1000).toFixed(0) : 132}K. The headline occupation.`, position: { top: "18%", left: "28%", maxWidth: "24ch" } },
  { prose: `Electricians: $${stats.elec_wage_2019 ? (stats.elec_wage_2019/1000).toFixed(0) : 60}K to $${stats.elec_wage_2024 ? (stats.elec_wage_2024/1000).toFixed(0) : 73}K. Not part of the debate. Should be.`, position: { top: "18%", left: "28%", maxWidth: "24ch" } },
  { prose: `A software developer earns roughly ${stats.wage_ratio ?? "1.8"}x an electrician. The gap is wide and not closing.`, position: { top: "18%", left: "28%", maxWidth: "24ch" } },
]));
```

The absolute gap between the two occupations is not closing. Software developers earn roughly twice what electricians earn nationally. But the *rate* of electrician wage growth is the signal. In counties with the heaviest data center construction — Loudoun County, Maricopa County, Licking County — electrician wages run materially above the national mean shown here. Spot wages in Northern Virginia for experienced commercial electricians reached $45 to $60 per hour as of 2024 — above the national mean — according to IBEW Local 26 wage data and contractor sourcing cited in industry reporting.

Two dynamics drive this. First, a structural undersupply: the pipeline from apprenticeship to journeyman electrician takes 4 to 5 years, and enrollment has not kept pace with demand. The Associated General Contractors of America's 2024 workforce survey found that 91% of firms reported difficulty finding qualified electricians. Second, geographic concentration: the demand spike is in specific counties, not distributed nationally.

## Where It Lands

Capital does not just have a dollar amount. It has a location. The data center industry is one of the most geographically concentrated sectors in the American economy.

The Northern Virginia cluster in Loudoun County and neighboring Fairfax, Prince William, and Manassas Park represents the most concentrated data center hub in the world. The geographic concentration creates the labor constraint: the demand for electricians, HVAC technicians, and data center operators is not distributed nationally. It concentrates in counties where the local labor pool cannot absorb the demand. This is why wage premiums in these counties exceed national averages, and why project timelines are slipping.

## The Feedback Loop

The labor market findings complete a causal loop that is not captured in either the standard AI-jobs debate or in standard infrastructure analysis.

The standard framing treats labor as an outcome: AI capex creates jobs. That is true. But the data shows a second dynamic: labor constraints are feeding back into capex deployment timelines. Electrician shortages in Northern Virginia and Phoenix are documented by contractors and not solved on the timescales relevant to hyperscaler expansion plans.

```js

  { prose: "Every data center requires 300-600 electricians for 24-36 months.", position: { top: "10%", right: "6%", maxWidth: "22ch" } },
  { prose: "The pipeline is constrained. Apprenticeship takes 4-5 years. 91% of firms report shortages.", position: { top: "10%", right: "6%", maxWidth: "22ch" } },
  { prose: "The loop closes. Labor constraints slow the buildout, which feeds back into capital deployment timelines.", position: { top: "10%", right: "6%", maxWidth: "22ch" } },

```

The implications connect directly to the DD-002 analysis: the interconnection queue is not the only bottleneck. Even if a data center clears its FERC interconnection study and gets a grid connection approved, it still needs to be built. Building it requires licensed electricians. If the electrician pipeline is constrained, approved grid connections sit idle — a form of stranded capital that does not appear in the queue data.

A back-of-envelope estimate: a large hyperscale data center campus (200 to 400 MW) requires 300 to 600 electricians for a 24 to 36 month construction period. Northern Virginia is developing multiple such campuses simultaneously. The IBEW apprenticeship pipeline produces roughly 35,000 new journeymen per year nationally (IBEW; National Electrical Contractors Association workforce projections) — and data centers are only one of many sectors competing for them. The math does not close without drawing workers from adjacent markets, which drives up wages, extends timelines, or both.

## What This Establishes

Three empirical findings:

1. **Tech sector employment acceleration is real and post-2023.** Computer Systems Design employment grew ${stats.tech_since_capex ?? "—"}% since January 2023, faster than any other major sector tracked in the FRED data.

2. **Electrician wages are rising significantly.** From $${stats.elec_wage_2019 ? stats.elec_wage_2019.toLocaleString() : "—"} to $${stats.elec_wage_2024 ? stats.elec_wage_2024.toLocaleString() : "—"} nationally (${stats.elec_wage_pct ?? "—"}%), with spot wages in cluster counties materially higher. This is a supply constraint, not just demand growth.

3. **Data center employment is hyperconcentrated.** ${stats.top_state ?? "California"} leads nationally. Five states capture the majority of operational employment. The concentration creates the labor constraint because demand exceeds local workforce capacity.

The labor constraint is not a policy problem that regulation can solve quickly. Electrician training takes 4 to 5 years. The demand surge began in 2023. The supply response, at best, starts arriving in 2027. In the interim, the constraint is real, binding, and a direct input to the capital deployment timelines analyzed in the rest of this series.

## Sources & Methodology

**Employment index.** BLS Current Employment Statistics (CES), monthly, seasonally adjusted. Series: CES6054150001 (Computer Systems Design), USCONS (Construction), USINFO (Information), MANEMP (Manufacturing). Via FRED / DuckDB research database. Index base: January 2020 = 100.

**Occupational wages.** BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS), annual national flat files, 2019-2024. Detailed occupation level (o_group = "detailed"). Annual mean wage in nominal dollars. SOC code 15-1252 (Software Developers) was renamed in the 2021 SOC revision — data available from 2021 onward only.

**State employment.** BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW), NAICS 518210 (Data Processing, Hosting, and Related Services), private sector, annual averages. States with suppressed employment (BLS disclosure code) are excluded.

**Data center locations.** 64 hyperscaler campuses compiled from public filings, company press releases, and industry reporting. Lat/lon coordinates from public sources. Capacity (MW) from company announcements where available, estimated where not.

**Workforce estimates.** National employment estimates for the unit chart are approximate, derived from BLS OES totals for the 15 tracked SOC codes. Actual employment varies by definition scope. The 1:1000 dot ratio is illustrative.

**Electrician pipeline.** IBEW apprenticeship data; AGC 2024 Workforce Survey (91% electrician shortage); NECA workforce projections. Data center electrician requirements (300-600 per campus) from contractor interviews and industry reporting.

*All data from public, freely accessible sources. Analysis: TZD Labs, March 2026.*
