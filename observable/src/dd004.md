# Who Pays for the AI Grid?

<div class="prose-lead">

A hyperscale data center campus costs $3-10 billion to build. It creates 30-50 permanent jobs. The grid upgrades it requires enter the utility rate base for 30-40 years. The private equity fund that may own the utility is designed to exit in 7.

That asymmetry is the subject of this article. Not whether data centers are good or bad for communities. Whether the cost allocation mechanism matches the liability structure. When the capital exits, someone is left holding infrastructure. The question is who.

</div>

---

## The Buyers

Private equity firms have been acquiring regulated electric and gas utilities since at least 2010. FERC filings show more than 90 fund-backed utility acquisitions in that period. The structure follows a pattern: a PE fund acquires a holding company that owns a regulated utility. The utility's rate base becomes a predictable cash-flow asset for the fund's investors. The fund lifecycle runs 7-10 years. The assets in the rate base amortize over 30-40.

The mechanism through which AI infrastructure becomes a ratepayer obligation runs through this ownership chain.

The PNM/Blackstone deal ($11.5B for New Mexico's largest electric utility) is the live case. But the pattern is structural, not company-specific. Any short-duration financial vehicle owning a regulated monopoly creates the same temporal mismatch between fund lifecycle and rate base asset life.

The central regulatory question: when a data center customer triggers grid upgrades that enter the rate base, and the utility's PE owner exits before those assets fully depreciate, which party bears the remaining cost?

### What communities actually get

The gap between investment dollars and permanent employment is not a criticism. It is arithmetic. A 300 MW hyperscale campus employs 30-50 permanent operations staff. Construction workers are real, but they disperse when the building is done.

It is "data centers vs. the alternative use of that grid connection, water allocation, industrial land, and public subsidy budget."

### The Virginia benchmark

Virginia hosts approximately ${stats.va_global_share_pct}% of global data center capacity, concentrated in Loudoun County. It is the longest-running example of what happens when data center concentration reaches critical mass.

The JLARC analysis (December 2024) provides the clearest picture of the community ledger. The industry contributes $${stats.va_dc_gdp_bn}B to Virginia GDP annually and ${stats.va_dc_jobs?.toLocaleString()} total jobs. ### The Indiana laboratory

Indiana has become the most active regulatory laboratory for the cost-liability question. Amazon's Project Rainier ($${stats.indiana_amazon_bn}B, 30 data center buildings) and Microsoft ($${stats.indiana_msft_bn}B) have committed $${stats.indiana_total_bn}B to the state. Indiana Michigan Power's peak load is projected to grow from ${stats.indiana_peak_2024_gw} GW to ${stats.indiana_peak_2030_gw} GW by 2030.

Four IURC proceedings between 2024 and 2026 establish the framework other states will copy or contest:

**Cause 46097** (Feb 2025): Created a tiered tariff for large load customers. Tier 1 (${stats.tier1_mw}+ MW): customer-specific equipment costs direct-assigned. Tier 2 (${stats.tier2_mw}+ MW): higher minimum demand charge. Both tiers: $${stats.demand_charge_kw}/kW/month minimum demand charge, $${stats.collateral_m}M collateral requirement. Critical asymmetry: generation capacity costs are socialized across all ratepayers.

**Cause 46217** (Nov 2025): Approved I&M's acquisition of the Oregon Clean Energy Center (${stats.oregon_mw} MW NGCC, 32 years remaining life to ~${stats.oregon_eol}). All costs into rate base.

**Cause 46301** (Jan 2026): EGR Plan approved ${stats.gas_ct_mw?.toLocaleString()} MW new gas combustion turbines and up to ${stats.clean_energy_mw?.toLocaleString()} MW clean energy. Total ICAP need: ${stats.total_icap_mw?.toLocaleString()} MW. All generation enters rate base; all ratepayers pay.

**Causes 46276/46305** (pending): Alternative Regulatory Plans for customer-specific clean capacity. Municipal intervenors (Marion, Fort Wayne, South Bend) signal ratepayer cost-shift concern.

The ${stats.gas_ct_mw?.toLocaleString()} MW of gas turbines have a 25-30 year cost amortization tail. The hyperscaler load commitments that justified them carry no obligation to remain customers for that duration.

## Who Holds the Bag

The liability taxonomy is the core of the cost allocation story. Every piece of infrastructure in the AI supply chain has a different owner, a different asset life, and a different party bearing the amortization risk.

A hyperscaler that exits I&M's service territory in 2035 leaves behind a 25-year amortization tail on gas turbines whose primary justification was their load commitment. Indiana's tariff attempts to address this with the $${stats.collateral_m}M collateral requirement and a ~$${stats.stranded_cost_1gw_bn?.toFixed(0)}B stranded cost exposure formula for early exit. But those provisions cover customer-specific infrastructure only. The generation capacity additions are socialized.

### The FERC lever

The highest-leverage regulatory variable in the AI infrastructure cost story is FERC Docket AD24-11. The May 2024 Policy Statement stated that cost-causation should apply to large loads and that upgrade costs "may be inappropriately socialized" under current rules. AI data centers present "novel challenges" for interconnection planning.

That Policy Statement has no regulatory force. It has not advanced to a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking or Final Rule. The existing rules remain in effect: costs can be socialized.

If FERC issues a Final Rule requiring cost-causation for large loads (as it did for generators in Order 2023), the cost-liability map changes substantially. The $${stats.transmission_cost_m}M in annual transmission costs currently recovered from all I&M ratepayers would instead be direct-assigned to the data center customers that triggered them.

Meanwhile, FERC Order 1920 (long-range transmission planning) is under legal challenge in the 8th Circuit. The AWS/Talen co-location proposal at Susquehanna was rejected by FERC in early 2025. Microsoft restarted Three Mile Island Unit 1 (now Crane Clean Energy Center) under a 20-year power purchase agreement with Constellation Energy, with the plant returning to commercial operation in September 2024. At least 15 states have introduced legislation addressing data center cost allocation.

The regulatory landscape is moving. The outcomes will determine whether the multi-decade infrastructure costs created by the AI buildout are borne by the customers who cause them or spread across every ratepayer in the service territory.

## Sources & Methodology

All numerical values in this article are loaded from the project's DuckDB database (`energy_data.source_citations`, `energy_data.dd004_iurc_cases`, `energy_data.dd004_pjm_zone_demand`). No values are hardcoded in prose.

**Primary sources:**
- JLARC Report 598, *Data Centers in Virginia* (Dec 2024) — Virginia subsidy and economic impact data
- IURC Cause 46097, Final Order (Feb 2025) — Large Load Service Rider tariff structure
- IURC Cause 46217, Final Order (Nov 2025) — Oregon NGCC acquisition into rate base
- IURC Cause 46301, Final Order (Jan 2026) — EGR Plan, ${stats.gas_ct_mw?.toLocaleString()} MW gas CT approval
- FERC Docket AD24-11, Policy Statement (May 2024) — Cost-causation for large loads
- PJM Load Analysis Subcommittee, Sep 2025 — Zone-level demand requests
- U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 5-Year Estimates (2023) — County-level socioeconomic data
- Company press releases and state economic development announcements (2010-2025) — Data center location data

**Data center location methodology:** The 64-facility dataset was compiled from public company announcements, state economic development office press releases, and local reporting. Each facility was geocoded and matched to Census county-level data using FIPS codes. ACS 5-year estimates provide median household income, poverty rate, and unemployment rate for each host county.

**Bill calculator disclaimer:** The interactive bill calculator is a simplified illustration. Actual rate impacts depend on utility-specific cost structures, approved rate cases, customer class allocation factors, and regulatory proceedings. The model uses representative parameters from Indiana Michigan Power (IURC Cause 46097) and EIA average residential rates.
